## Why AI audits often fail in SMEs

In many SMEs, AI audits start with ambitious ideas, then dissolve into long workshops, non-comparable tests, and no hard decisions. The issue is rarely tooling. It is usually the lack of an **operating frame**: who decides, using which criteria, and on what timeline.

A useful audit is not a report. It is a **decision system** that must answer three clear questions:

- Where do we actually lose time today?
- Which use cases produce visible gains in under 90 days?
- What deployment sequence minimizes adoption risk?

## Week 1: map the costly workflows

Week one is not technical. It is operational. The objective is to expose recurring friction: duplicate entry, manual follow-ups, scattered information retrieval, and repeated context handoffs between teams.

In practice, map 5 to 8 key processes (sales, support, admin, document production, reporting). For each one, measure:

- Weekly volume
- Average handling time
- Rework/correction rate
- Dependency on a single key person

This baseline prevents a common trap: prioritizing use cases that look sophisticated but barely move business outcomes.

## Week 2: score use cases with one shared rubric

At this stage, teams usually have 15 to 25 ideas. You need a short list that can be executed. A simple scoring model is enough when applied consistently:

1. **Impact**: hours recovered, quality, customer speed
2. **Feasibility**: data quality, integration constraints, security
3. **Adoption**: change-management effort
4. **Risk**: error exposure, compliance implications

Avoid overweighting “wow factor” and underweighting maintenance burden. In SMEs, high-performing use cases often remove repeated micro-friction rather than attempting a full-system redesign.

## Week 3: prototype in real conditions

A useful prototype must run with real tools (CRM, calendar, messaging, document stack), not in an isolated sandbox. Otherwise, you validate a demo, not a production path.

The goal is binary:

- Clear measurable gain: proceed to industrialization.
- Fragile result despite iteration: stop early and reallocate effort.

This stop discipline is critical. Audits should protect budget as much as they identify opportunity.

## Week 4: decide, sequence, and assign ownership

The final week locks execution fundamentals:

- Top 3 automations to launch
- Phase-1 scope (3 to 6 weeks)
- Success metrics (before/after)
- Internal roles (sponsor, owner, key users)
- Review cadence (short weekly, strategic monthly)

Governance must stay lightweight. SMEs do not need a 12-person steering committee. They need a **minimal framework** that keeps momentum without bureaucracy.

## KPIs that matter

Track four indicators from day one:

- Hours recovered per week
- Lead-time reduction
- Error/rework rate
- User-team satisfaction

These KPIs connect internal perception (“this feels better”) to measurable business value.

## What the audit must deliver

A strong SME AI audit should produce a document readable in 10 minutes:

- Target processes
- Quantified baseline
- Prioritized use cases
- Conservative ROI estimate
- Deployment plan with risks and mitigations

## Conclusion

A successful 30-day SME AI audit follows one principle: **fewer slides, more decisions**. With strict prioritization and real-context prototyping, AI becomes operational gain rather than an endless transversal initiative.
